Fallacy #3 March 20, 2026

We Need a Big Event

The Gala That Ate Your Fundraising Program

John Jalsevac, PhD

Part 3 of Everything You Believe About Fundraising is Wrong: 9 Mistakes That Are Killing Your Results

Every nonprofit has one. The annual gala. The black-tie dinner. The golf tournament. The silent auction with the donated weekend at a cottage and the signed hockey jersey and the basket of artisanal soaps.

It consumes months of planning. It exhausts your staff. It burns out your volunteers. And when it's over, everyone collapses and says, "Well, that was a success. We raised $80,000!"

But did you?

Let's look more carefully. Because the belief that your organization needs a big fundraising event to survive is one of the most expensive, most exhausting, and most seductive mistakes in the nonprofit world. Not because events are inherently bad. They aren't. But because the way most organizations think about events is fundamentally confused, and that confusion is quietly devouring resources that could be producing far better results elsewhere.

The math no one wants to do

Let's take a typical community nonprofit gala. It's an annual event. Tickets are $150 a head. You sell 200 seats. There's a silent auction. A live auction. A paddle raise. At the end of the night, the gross revenue is $80,000.

The board is thrilled. The executive director sends a thank-you email with a photo of the packed room. Everyone agrees it was a wonderful evening. The number $80,000 goes into the annual report.

But now let's do the math that almost nobody does.

Direct costs you can see:

Venue rental: $5,000. Catering at $65 a head for 200 guests: $13,000. Audio-visual and staging: $2,500. Printed materials, signage, centrepieces: $1,500. Entertainment or speaker fee: $2,000. Online auction platform: $800. Miscellaneous (photography, coat check, parking, insurance): $1,200.

That's $26,000 in direct costs. Your $80,000 is now $54,000 net. Not bad, you might think.

But you haven't counted the expensive part yet.

The costs that never appear on the budget:

Your development director spent approximately 400 hours over five months planning this event. At a fully loaded compensation cost of $40 per hour (salary plus benefits), that's $16,000 in staff time.

Your executive director spent 60 hours on event-related tasks: securing sponsors, calling table captains, reviewing the program, rehearsing remarks. At their compensation rate, that's another $4,500.

Your marketing coordinator spent 80 hours on invitations, social media promotion, auction catalogue design, and follow-up communications. That's $2,400.

Three board members each spent 30 hours over several months on the event committee: soliciting auction items, selling tables, coordinating logistics. If you valued their time at even a modest $50 per hour (and many of your board members bill far more than that professionally), that's $4,500 in volunteer labour.

A dozen other volunteers contributed another 200 hours collectively on the night itself and in preparation: $10,000 at the same rate.

Add it up. The staff and volunteer time alone totals roughly $37,400. Combined with the $26,000 in direct costs, the true cost of your gala was approximately $63,400.

Your $80,000 event netted $16,600.

That's not a fundraising triumph. That's a cost-per-dollar-raised of nearly $0.80.

For context, Graham-Pelton Consulting has noted that "industry data shows that events cost an organization 50 cents for every dollar raised, making it the most expensive form of fundraising when compared to annual giving, major gifts, and corporate and foundation giving." And that's the average. Many galas, once you account for the hidden costs, perform even worse.

Meanwhile, the Association of Fundraising Professionals reports that major gift fundraising costs between $0.05 and $0.20 per dollar raised. Some practitioners report a return of 30:1 or higher when major gifts are done well. A well-run annual fund appeal costs roughly $0.20 per dollar. Your gala is costing you four to sixteen times more per dollar than the alternatives.

And we haven't even discussed opportunity cost yet.

The real price: what you didn't do

Here is where the analysis gets painful.

Those 400 hours your development director spent on the gala? That's 400 hours she did not spend meeting with your top 30 donors. She didn't make the cultivation calls. She didn't write the personal notes. She didn't prepare the solicitation briefings. She didn't build the prospect pipeline. She didn't launch the planned giving conversation with the retired couple who've been giving $500 a year for a decade and have no children.

Those 60 hours your executive director spent on the event? That's 60 hours he wasn't sitting across the table from a potential $25,000 donor, sharing the organization's vision over coffee.

According to the Bloomerang Foundation's analysis, a development professional who redirects even half of their event-planning hours toward major gift cultivation can generate three to five times the net revenue of the event itself.

Think about that. If your development director had spent just 200 of those 400 gala hours on personal donor meetings instead, conducting roughly 8 meetings per month over five months, and if even a quarter of those meetings resulted in a gift of $2,000 or more, that's $20,000 in major gifts. With a cost-per-dollar-raised of perhaps $0.10. Net revenue: approximately $18,000. From half the time.

The gala didn't just cost you $63,400 in direct and indirect expenses. It cost you every relationship that wasn't cultivated, every donor meeting that didn't happen, every major gift conversation that was postponed because "we'll get to that after the gala."

And there's a human cost, too. Graham-Pelton has noted that the event-planning burden on development staff was a significant contributor to burnout during the Great Resignation. When your development director spends half her year as an event planner, she's not doing the work she was hired to do, and she knows it. The resulting frustration and exhaustion drive talented fundraisers out of the profession entirely.

This is the real price of the big event. Not what it costs to run, but what it prevents you from doing.

Why organizations cling to events anyway

If the math is this unfavourable, why do nonprofits keep doing it?

Because events solve an emotional problem, not a financial one.

Events feel like fundraising without the discomfort of actually asking. When you sell a gala ticket, you're not really asking for a donation. You're selling an evening out. When you run a silent auction, you're not asking anyone to give sacrificially. You're offering them a deal on a weekend getaway. The entire apparatus of the big event is designed, often unconsciously, to avoid the one thing that actually raises serious money: looking someone in the eye and asking them to invest in your mission.

Dan Pallotta captured this brilliantly in his TED Talk when he asked: what if the bake sale only netted $71, but the professional fundraising enterprise netted $71 million? We've been taught that the bake sale with its 5% overhead is morally superior to the professional operation with 40% overhead. But we're looking at the wrong number. The question is not how much it cost. The question is how much it accomplished.

This connects directly back to Belief #1 in this series. If fundraising feels embarrassing, events are the perfect escape. They create a busy, visible, socially acceptable structure that looks like fundraising but allows everyone involved to avoid the vulnerable, personal act of making a direct ask.

Events also create the illusion of community engagement. A packed ballroom feels like momentum. A sold-out event feels like success. And in fairness, there is real value in bringing supporters together in a room. But that value is not primarily financial. It's relational and cultural. And the problem arises when organizations confuse the two.

There's also institutional inertia. Once an organization has held an annual gala for five or ten or twenty years, cancelling it feels like admitting defeat. Board members who joined specifically because they enjoy the social cachet of the event will resist. Sponsors who've supported the event for years may feel slighted. The event develops its own gravitational pull, independent of whether it actually serves the organization's fundraising goals.

What events are actually good for

Here is where I want to be careful, because I'm not arguing that nonprofits should never hold events. I'm arguing that they should understand what events are actually good for, and stop pretending they're something they're not.

Events are excellent for three things:

Community building. A well-designed event brings your supporters together in a room where they can meet each other, feel connected to the mission, and experience a sense of belonging. This is genuinely valuable. It strengthens the emotional bonds between donors and the organization. It creates a shared identity. It gives people a story to tell their friends.

Celebration and gratitude. An event can be a powerful way to thank your supporters, to celebrate milestones, to honour long-time donors or volunteers, and to publicly mark what the community has accomplished together. Gratitude events, where the purpose is explicitly stewardship rather than solicitation, can be some of the most effective things an organization does.

Visibility and awareness. Events put your organization in front of people who may not otherwise encounter it. A well-attended, well-run event signals credibility and momentum. It can introduce potential donors to the mission in a low-pressure setting.

Notice what's not on this list: primary revenue generation. Events can supplement a fundraising program. They should never be the foundation of one. The moment your gala becomes the main source of your fundraising revenue, you've built your house on sand.

The healthiest organizations I've worked with treat their events as cultivation tools, not fundraising tools. The event creates the warmth. The personal follow-up afterward creates the gift.

There's a striking case study that illustrates this. CCS Fundraising documented how Anne Arundel Medical Center transformed its annual gala by treating it explicitly as a major gift cultivation platform rather than a standalone fundraiser. By using the event to identify and qualify major gift prospects, then following up with a structured cultivation and solicitation process, they increased their conversion rate of gala attendees to major donors from 0.5% to nearly 20% in two years. The gala itself didn't change dramatically. What changed was what happened after the gala: the personal meetings, the cultivation calls, the relationship-building that turned an evening of goodwill into lasting philanthropic partnerships.

The power of the small gathering

If the big gala is often a poor financial investment, what's the alternative?

Consider the small, intimate event. A wine and cheese evening at a board member's home. A dinner for twelve at a local restaurant. A living room conversation with a carefully chosen group of people who care about the mission.

These gatherings cost almost nothing to produce. A few hundred dollars for food and wine. A few hours of planning. No venue rental. No AV. No auction catalogue. No five months of committee meetings.

But what they accomplish is disproportionate to their cost.

In a room of twelve people, real conversations happen. Your executive director can share the organization's story with the kind of depth and vulnerability that's impossible from a podium in front of 200 people. A board member can introduce a friend to the mission in a setting that feels personal rather than transactional. A long-time donor can hear directly from a program beneficiary. Questions can be asked and answered. Connections can form.

Penelope Burk's research found that 67% of donors who attended cultivation events credited the event as the reason they made their gift. And a third of those attendees went on to make unsolicited gifts, giving without even being asked, simply because the event deepened their connection to the work.

This is the paradox. The smaller the event, the larger the potential return. Not because you're extracting more money per person, but because you're building the kind of relationship that makes a significant gift feel natural rather than forced.

A board member who hosts a gathering in their home is doing something far more powerful than selling a table at a gala. They're lending their personal credibility to the organization. They're saying to their friends and colleagues: "This matters to me. I want you to know about it." That personal endorsement, delivered in the intimacy of a living room with a glass of wine, is worth more than any silent auction paddle.

And after the gathering, when you follow up with a personal note and then a phone call and then a coffee meeting, the ask doesn't feel like an ask. It feels like the natural next step in a relationship that's already been established.

Reimagining your event strategy

I'm not suggesting you cancel your gala tomorrow (though for some organizations, that might be exactly the right move). I'm suggesting you rethink what it's for.

If your event is your primary fundraising vehicle, something has gone wrong. It means you're spending your most expensive hours on your least efficient fundraising channel. It means your development director is an event planner when they should be a relationship builder. It means your board is selling tables when they should be opening doors.

Here's what a healthier event strategy looks like:

Stop counting your event as a fundraising success based on gross revenue. Calculate the true cost, including staff time, volunteer time, and opportunity cost. If the cost-per-dollar-raised is above $0.40, you have a problem.

Reframe your big annual event as a community celebration, not a revenue engine. Budget it as a stewardship expense. If it breaks even or generates a modest surplus while strengthening donor relationships, that's a success. If it's consuming half your development staff's annual capacity, it's not a success regardless of the gross revenue number.

Shift your development director's time toward personal donor cultivation. Every hour freed from event planning is an hour that can be invested in relationships that will produce three to five times the return.

Introduce small, intimate cultivation events throughout the year. Four gatherings of 10 to 15 people, hosted in board members' homes or at local restaurants, will almost certainly produce more net revenue than one gala for 200. And they'll build deeper, more durable donor relationships in the process.

Use your events to set up the ask, not to make it. The event creates the warmth, the connection, the sense of shared purpose. The personal follow-up turns that warmth into a gift.

The hardest question

There's one question that boards and executive directors rarely ask, but should: if we didn't hold this event, what would we do with the time?

If the answer is "nothing," then the event is fine. Keep it.

But if the answer is "we'd meet with more donors, write more personal notes, make more cultivation calls, build a prospect pipeline, launch a major gifts program, train the board to open doors," then you already know what to do. The event isn't raising money. It's preventing you from raising money.

The belief that your organization needs a big event to fundraise successfully is, in most cases, a comforting fiction. It lets everyone stay busy without doing the harder, quieter, more personal work that actually builds a sustainable fundraising program.

The organizations that raise the most money are not the ones with the biggest galas. They are the ones with the deepest relationships. And relationships are built not in ballrooms, but in living rooms, across coffee tables, and over glasses of wine, one conversation at a time.
