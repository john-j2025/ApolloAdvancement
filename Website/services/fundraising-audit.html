Sound Familiar?

You know your organization could raise more. You're just not sure where to start.

Maybe you're an executive director who's been handling fundraising on top of everything else. Maybe you're a board member who knows the organization needs to be doing more, but every conversation about money ends in silence. Maybe you inherited a donor list from a predecessor and you're not even sure what's in it.

The problem isn't effort. It's clarity.

Are we leaving money on the table? Why do donors give once and never come back? Is our board supposed to be doing more? Should we be running another event, or are events draining us? Is our donor base growing or shrinking? Are we spending too much on the gala and not enough on thank-you calls?
You're tired of piecing together advice from webinars, blog posts, and conferences. You need someone to look at your actual data, talk to your actual people, and tell you what's really going on.

That's what a fundraising audit does.

What It Is

My audit. Your roadmap.

The Fundraising Audit is a comprehensive, six-to-eight-week diagnostic that examines your fundraising program from the inside out. I review your donor data, interview your leadership, survey your board, assess your communications, and benchmark your results against sector standards.

This isn't a report full of generic best practices. Every finding is grounded in your actual data, your actual donors, and your actual capacity. I've done this work from the inside: as a headmaster who grew a school's revenue from $280K to $600K, and as a campaign director who built a donor base from 80 to 325. I know what it looks like when an organization is sitting on untapped potential, because I've been the one who had to find it.

The audit gives you the honest picture. The strategic plan gives you the roadmap. Together, they tell you exactly where to focus your limited time and resources for the greatest return.

The Seven Areas of the Audit

We look at everything. Then we tell you what matters most.

01 Organizational culture and leadership alignment.

Is fundraising treated as central to the mission, or as a necessary evil? Does leadership participate in donor cultivation? Is the development function set up to succeed, or set up to struggle alone?

02 Board engagement and giving.

What percentage of your board gives? Do board members open doors to potential donors? We survey your board anonymously to understand their comfort level, beliefs, and willingness to engage.

03 Donor data analysis.

Retention rates, acquisition and attrition trends, revenue by source, giving-level distribution, upgrade potential, and lapsed donor profiles. We benchmark against the AFP Fundraising Effectiveness Project and peer organizations through CRA or IRS filings.

04 Fundraising programs.

A channel-by-channel review of your annual fund, major gifts, events, grants, and planned giving. For each: structure, results, cost-per-dollar-raised, and strategic rationale. Which programs are earning their keep, and which are consuming resources without proportional return?

05 Communications and stewardship.

How quickly are gifts acknowledged? How often do you communicate with donors between asks? Are your appeals donor-centered or organization-centered? We review every touchpoint from the first thank-you letter to the annual report.

06 Prospect pipeline.

Do you have a formal prospect list? How are new potential donors identified? How many face-to-face donor meetings happen each month? For most organizations, this is where the largest untapped opportunity lives.

07 Belief diagnostic.

This is what makes an Apollo Advancement audit different. We map what we find in the data back to the underlying beliefs producing those results. Beliefs like "fundraising is embarrassing" or "if we do good work, the money will come" shape behavior in ways that no amount of tactical advice will fix. Naming them is the first step toward changing them.

The cost of not knowing is another year of guessing.

Without a clear picture of your fundraising program, every decision is a gamble. You invest in events that may not be paying for themselves. You lose donors you could have kept. You leave major gift conversations on the table because nobody is sure whose job it is to have them. Another year passes, and the board asks the same questions they asked last year.

The Strategic Plan

A roadmap your team can actually follow.

The strategic plan translates the audit findings into a concrete, twelve-month action plan organized into three tiers:

Months 1-3Immediate priorities.

High-impact changes your team can make right away. Fixing the acknowledgment process, establishing 100% board giving, launching a regular donor communication cadence, cleaning your CRM data, and identifying upgrade candidates from your existing donor base.

Months 3-6Near-term priorities.

Structural work that builds a durable program. A written case for support, a structured annual fund with multiple touchpoints, major gift cultivation with your top 20-50 donors, board fundraising training, and a stewardship plan by donor level.

Months 6-12Medium-term investments.

Longer-horizon work that compounds over time. Diversifying revenue streams, developing planned giving marketing, building a formal prospect research process, evaluating event ROI, and upgrading systems where needed.

The plan includes revenue projections tied to each recommendation, so your board can see not just what to do but what the expected return looks like. It is designed to be achievable with your current team and resources.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions.

What does a nonprofit fundraising audit include?

A fundraising audit reviews every dimension of your development program: donor database health and segmentation, giving trends and lapse rates, current communications and their effectiveness, annual fund structure, board and volunteer engagement, major gift activity and pipeline, campaign history, and your overall case for support. The goal is to identify what's working, what isn't, and what's missing entirely.

How long does a fundraising audit take?

A typical audit takes four to eight weeks from initial data gathering to delivery of the final report, depending on the size and complexity of your program and how quickly stakeholders can participate in interviews and review sessions.

What's the difference between a fundraising audit and a strategic plan?

The audit is the diagnostic: examining what you have and assessing its health. The strategic plan is the prescription: what to do next, in what order, and why. Together they give you an honest picture of your current program and a realistic roadmap for improving it. You can't build a good strategy without first understanding the foundation you're building on.

When should a nonprofit do a fundraising audit?

The most common triggers are: fundraising revenue that has plateaued or declined, an executive director transition, a board that wants to professionalize the development function, or preparation for a major campaign. It's also valuable when leadership suspects there's untapped potential in the donor base but isn't sure where to find it.

Do I need to be local to work with you?

No. I'm based in Kawartha Lakes, Ontario, but I serve nonprofits across Canada and the United States. Interviews and presentations can be conducted in person or by video, depending on your preference and location.
