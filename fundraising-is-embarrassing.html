Wrong Belief #1 March 13, 2026

Fundraising is Embarrassing

The Original Sin That Poisons Everything Else

John Jalsevac, PhD

Part 1 of Everything You Believe About Fundraising is Wrong: 9 Mistakes That Are Killing Your Results

"I'm sick of apologizing for being in charge of raising money."

That's the opening line of one of the most widely shared documents in the nonprofit world: Sasha Dichter's "Manifesto in Defense of Raising Money," written in 2008.

At the time, Dichter was the Chief Innovation Officer at Acumen, a pioneering organization fighting global poverty. He believed in the work. He was good at it. And yet when a mentor heard he was taking on a fundraising role, he was told: "You can do better than that."

Note: this wasn't an outsider. It was an experienced leader inside the non-profit sector, treating fundraising as a career stain.

In his manifesto, Dichter presented a simple, furious response: if you've given your life to solving a problem that matters, why are you ashamed to ask other people to help you solve it?

"You're devoting your life, your spirit, your energy, your faith into making the vision you have of a better future into a reality. So why are you so scared to ask people for money?"

In my experience, this is one of the most important, transformative questions non-profit leaders can ask themselves. Transformative, because if they answer it correctly, it will literally change everything about the way they approach fundraising.

The belief that poisons the well

Here's what I've seen, again and again, working with nonprofits: the organizations that struggle most with fundraising don't lack for good programs, passionate staff, or worthy missions. What they lack is a healthy relationship with fundraising itself.

And I don't mean they lack technique. I mean something deeper.

They believe, at a gut level, that fundraising is…embarrassing. That it's awkward. That it's something you have to do, so that you can get around to doing the real work of the organization. That donors are people to be managed, not partners to be cherished.

I call this belief the original sin of nonprofit fundraising. Not because it's the most dramatic mistake an organization can make, but because it's the one from which so many others flow.

If you believe fundraising is embarrassing, you won't get good at it. Full stop.

You won't invest in learning how to do it well. You won't practice. You won't read the books or listen to the podcasts that might open your eyes to just how fascinating fundraising can be. You won't build the systems and processes that effective fundraising requires, because building those systems would mean treating fundraising as something worth doing well.

If you believe fundraising is embarrassing, you'll default to the approaches that feel least like asking. You'll hold expensive, time-consuming events. You'll run bake sales and car washes and silent auctions. You'll send out mass mailings with no personal touch. You'll do anything, anything, to avoid sitting across the table from a real person and making a genuine, heartfelt ask.

If you believe fundraising is embarrassing, your executive director won't do it. Your board won't do it. Your volunteers will treat it as the one part of their commitment they'd rather skip. And the one or two staff members who are assigned to fundraising will carry the weight of everyone else's discomfort on their shoulders.

If you believe fundraising is embarrassing, your donors will feel it. People are remarkably attuned to the emotional signals of the person sitting across from them. A fundraiser who approaches a donor with apology in their voice, with a sense that they're imposing, with the unspoken message that this whole thing is a bit beneath everyone involved, will get exactly the response they expect: polite deflection.

As one writer in the field put it: "If you're not comfortable enough in yourself or your program to ask someone for money, why would they be comfortable enough to give it to you?"

The research backs this up

This isn't just anecdotal. Studies consistently show that people underestimate how willing others are to help when asked directly.

Research involving volunteers for the Leukemia and Lymphoma Society found that fundraisers overestimated the number of people they'd need to ask to reach their fundraising goal by 50 percent. The average fundraiser thought they'd need to ask about 210 people. The actual number was about 122.

People wanted to give. They were waiting to be asked. The fundraisers just couldn't believe it.

The problem isn't out there with the donors. The problem is inside the organization.

The reframe that changes everything

So here's the question: what if fundraising isn't an imposition at all?

What if, instead of thinking of yourself as someone with their hand out, you thought of yourself as someone offering an invitation?

Not an invitation to a gala. Not a guilt trip disguised as a tax receipt. But a genuine invitation to invest (using that word alone can cause an immediate and dramatic reframe) in something that matters.

This is the reframe that transforms fundraising from something you endure into something you can actually take pride in. When you ask someone to support your organization, you are not asking for a favor. You are offering them a chance to be part of something meaningful. You are connecting them to a cause, a community, a mission that is making the world better.

"People think that asking for money is all about asking for money. It is and it isn't. Most of the time it is about inspiring someone to see the world the way you do, and convincing them that the change you are trying to make is one worth investing in."

Dan Pallotta, whose TED Talk "The Way We Think About Charity is Dead Wrong" has been viewed millions of times, has spent decades hammering the same point from a different angle: the nonprofit sector will never reach its potential as long as it treats the act of raising money as something shameful.

We don't expect Apple to apologize for selling iPhones. We shouldn't expect nonprofits to apologize for raising the money they need to change the world.

A spirituality of fundraising

One of the most widely read books on fundraising takes an even more dramatic stance.

Catholic priest Henri Nouwen was one of the most widely read spiritual writers of the twentieth century. He was a professor at Notre Dame, Yale, and Harvard who spent the last decade of his life living and working with people with intellectual disabilities at the L'Arche Daybreak community in Richmond Hill, Ontario.

Shortly before his death in 1996, he gave a talk that was later published as a small booklet called A Spirituality of Fundraising. It has quietly become one of the most important texts in the field, far beyond the Catholic world in which Nouwen lived.

Nouwen's argument goes to the root of why fundraising makes people uncomfortable, and it offers a reframe so complete that it doesn't just make fundraising tolerable. It makes it sacred.

"Fundraising is precisely the opposite of begging. When we seek to raise funds we are not saying, 'Please, could you help us out because lately it's been hard.' Rather, we are declaring, 'We have a vision that is amazing and exciting. We are inviting you to invest yourself through the resources that God has given you—your energy, your prayers, and your money—in this work to which God has called us.'"

Nouwen understood that the embarrassment around fundraising comes from a deeply distorted picture of what's actually happening in the exchange.

This is why Nouwen insisted that fundraising is "not a necessary but unpleasant activity to support spiritual things." It is itself a spiritual act. It is the act of inviting another human being into a vision bigger than themselves. And that act, done well, is not an imposition. It is a gift.

From apology to invitation

I have seen this shift happen in real time with organizations I've worked with. The moment a board chair stops thinking of donor meetings as something painful and starts thinking of them as a chance to invite someone into something meaningful, everything changes. The conversations get better. The relationships deepen. The gifts get larger. Not because the technique improved, but because the belief changed.

But something else happens when this shift takes hold. Once people inside an organization stop treating fundraising as something separate from the mission, they start to see how it belongs everywhere.

The program director starts paying attention to the stories emerging from the work, not because someone told her to collect them for the next appeal, but because she realizes that sharing those stories is part of the mission. She sees that every life changed, every community strengthened, every person served is not just a programmatic outcome. It is evidence that something good is happening. And sharing that evidence is not marketing. It is ministry.

Telling the story of a life changed, a community strengthened, a child who found their footing produces hope. It generates gratitude. It spreads evidence of human goodness in a world that is hungry for exactly that. In other words: telling the story of the work is itself a form of doing good.

The finance team starts thinking about how to show donors the real impact of their dollars. The communications team starts writing not to fill a newsletter but to invite people deeper into the mission. The volunteers become ambassadors.

Fundraising stops being a department and becomes a way of life. A way of being that says: what we are doing here matters, and we are not going to be shy about inviting the world to be part of it.

That is the shift. Not from bad fundraising to good fundraising. From embarrassment to conviction. From apology to invitation.

And once it happens, everything else follows.
